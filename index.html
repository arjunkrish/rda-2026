HMGP 7622: Rethinking Data Analysis

A researcher’s guide to avoiding missteps and misuse

Spring 2026 | Tuesdays 2:00-3:30 PM | AHSB 4160
February 17 – May 5, 2026

Course description

Rethinking data analysis is an advanced short course designed to:

  1. Discuss common misunderstandings & typical errors in the practice of data analysis.
  2. Provide a mental toolkit for critically thinking about analysis methods and results.

Classes will involve lectures, discussions, hands-on exercises, and homework about concepts critical to the day-to-day use and consumption of quantitative/computational techniques for working with data.

Note: Open to graduate students for credit. Trainees and professionals at all stages are welcome to unofficially audit this course.

Prerequisites

  1. Introductory knowledge of statistics and probability, and
  2. Introductory experience with data wrangling, analysis, and visualization using R or Python. Pre-written R & Python code will be provided and learners will be expected to understand, modify, and run the code. These are strict requirements for students taking the course for credit and a strong recommendation for those unofficially auditing it.

The course website will contain all the information related to this course including pointers to free online preparatory materials.

Weekly topics overview

  • Week 1: Scientific method and hypothesis formation • Recognizing when study designs fail • Critical evaluation of research claims • Cognitive biases in data interpretation • Statistical intuition vs. blind rule-following

  • Week 2: Uncertainty laundering • Confidence intervals vs. prediction intervals • Bootstrap methods • Error propagation • How missing data gets mishandled • The broken ritual of normality testing

  • Week 3: What P-values measure (and what they don’t) • P-hacking: subtle and obvious forms • Multiple testing problem • Publication bias and file drawer problem • Conflicts of interest and analytical flexibility

  • Week 4: Type I and Type II errors in practice • Statistical power: the underpowered majority • The “Sizeless Stare”: statistical vs. practical significance • Sample size planning: beyond ritual

  • Week 5: Pseudoreplication: getting the experimental unit wrong • Effective sample size • Confounding variables • Batch effects and technical artifacts

  • Week 6: Circular analysis: dipping into the data twice • Regression to the mean • Sampling biases: who’s missing and why it matters • Pre-registration: the solution to many problems

  • Week 7: Bayesian vs. frequentist: different questions • Base rate neglect • Prior specification • Communicating probabilistic results

  • Week 8: Correlation vs. causation • Spurious correlations • Effect modification and interaction • Model misspecification • Appropriate measures for different data types

  • Week 9: Curse of dimensionality • Overfitting and model selection • Effective evaluation strategies • Feature selection and multiple testing • Privacy risks in high-dimensional data

  • Week 10: Principles of effective data visualization • Common visualization mistakes and deceptions • Communicating uncertainty visually • Audience-appropriate statistical communication

  • Week 11: Data management and documentation • Code organization and version control • Reproducible analysis workflows • Collaborative research practices • Responsible data sharing practices • Ethical transparency through documentation

  • Week 12: The promise and peril of AI for data analysis • AI failure modes in statistical analysis • Using AI responsibly and effectively • Building AI literacy for data analysis • Ethical dimensions of AI-assisted analysis

Frequently asked questions

Why was this course developed?

As many parts of biological and biomedical research are becoming data-intensive, typical graduate training involves courses in coding and statistics. However, students face a significant challenge when trying to apply these learnings to novel research questions and complex datasets. This requires knowing:

  1. What the practical definitions of various concepts and their relationships are
  2. When and why they are applied in certain situations and not others
  3. What is a robust sequence of actions to take when applying them to data
  4. How to judiciously interpret the results
  5. How to clearly and transparently communicate the findings

Currently, most students learn these ideas only by piecing together a mental model of “acceptable/standard/best practices” based on bits of information from mentors, peers, and published papers.

This course addresses this challenge by focusing on common misunderstandings and typical errors in statistical data analysis practice, providing a mental toolkit for critical thinking about statistical methods and results.

Will this course teach me concepts in statistics?

There are already plenty of existing courses at CU that teach introductory, intermediate, and advanced statistics. Search the Academic Catalog using “statistics” under Keyword Search to get the full list. Statistics at the level of BMSC 7820 or BIOS 6601 is strongly recommended before taking this course.

This is a non-traditional course aimed at discussing what happens—issues that crop up and nuances that become relevant—when ideas from traditional courses are applied to actual research, messy data, and real-world problems.

Why is coding (in languages like R or Python) a prerequisite for this course?

The goal is to create opportunities for developing strong intuition behind many concepts in data analysis. This course takes the approach of using programming to implement statistical ideas on real/simulated data and observe the results to build intuition behind various concepts.

If you would like introductory courses in programming (in R or Python) and statistical analysis, please check out BSBT 6805, MOLB 7900/7910, or BIOS 6642/6682.

What are some specific coding skills I would need for this course?

You will be writing code to: - Read in datasets and wrangle them into a convenient format - Call common statistical functions from standard packages/libraries - Implement simulations/tests (random number generation, for/while loops) - Make plots (scatterplot, histograms, boxplots, etc.)

For R users: - tidyverse (readr, dplyr, ggplot2) - Calculating summary statistics (mean/median, std-deviation/variance, correlation) - Generating random numbers (runif, rnorm) - Writing for & while loops

For Python users: - pandas (data wrangling) - seaborn (data visualization) - numpy (summary statistics, random numbers) - Writing for & while loops

You can find recommended free online resources for learning these skills on the course resources page.

I’m a postdoc (or research staff) and would like to just attend the class—no need for credits. Is that possible?

Yes! I’m open to having postdocs, research/staff scientists, and faculty members audit this course for free. The only things I ask in return are your active engagement in the class and course materials along with providing constructive feedback.
